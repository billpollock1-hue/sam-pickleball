Chapter One

The Challenge: From Perception to Evidence

Players in the SAM shootout have raised concerns about court competitiveness — too many lopsided games, courts that feel mismatched.

Perception is a starting point, but it is not enough to diagnose the problem or evaluate solutions. We need an objective metric: a way to measure the skill gap between teams in any given game, consistently, across thousands of games and multiple years.

Fortunately, we have exactly the raw material that requires: the historical SAM shootout data from Pickleball Den.

Chapter Two

The Metric: Modified Elo

Elo is a rating system originally developed for chess and widely used in competitive sports. Every player starts at 1,000. After each game, all four players’ ratings update based on the result versus what the model predicted.

Your team’s rating is the average of you and your partner. The bigger the rating gap between two teams, the more confidently the model expects the stronger side to win.

Two club-specific adjustments — covered in the appendix — make sure long tenure carries no built-in advantage and new players reach an accurate rating quickly.

3
Three Factors Drive Every Update
1 · RESULTWinning earns points; losing costs points.
2 · MARGINAn 11–2 win moves ratings more than an 11–9 win.
3 · OPPONENT STRENGTHBeating a strong team earns more than beating a weak one.
In observed SAM games, the higher-rated team wins 57% at a 100-point gap, 66% at 200, and 78% at 300.

Chapter Three

Does the Model Actually Work?

A rating is only useful if it predicts real outcomes. So before drawing any conclusions, we checked the model against every rated game in the dataset.

The pattern is exactly what a healthy rating system should show: the bigger the pre-game rating gap between two teams, the more often the favorite wins — and the more lopsided the score gets.

Small gaps produce coin-flip games decided by a point or two. Big gaps produce blowouts. The facing page shows the full relationship.

Chapter Four

The Evidence: A Growing Competitiveness Problem

With a trustworthy measuring stick, we can now measure match quality directly. “Match gap” is the rating difference between the two teams in a game — smaller means more evenly matched.

The trend is unmistakable. The average match gap has nearly doubled since early 2022, and games that qualify as closely matched — a gap under 200 points — have fallen from 86% of all games to 56%.

Mismatched games are no longer rare exceptions. They are now the norm for roughly 4 in 10 matches.

Chapter Five

The Root Cause: A Wider Player Pool

The driver is not a shortage of strong players — it is that the SAM player pool has grown dramatically more diverse in skill.

Today’s leaderboard spans 49 active players — those with at least 24 rated games played within the past 180 days. The rating spread across that group is 1,300 points, top to bottom.

At that dispersion, the strongest player would be expected to beat the bottom of the leaderboard well over 99% of the time. Placing 12–20 players of this range onto 3–5 courts is genuinely hard — and the current assignment method wasn’t built for it.

Chapter Six

How Courts Are Assigned Today

DEN builds Session 1 courts from two numbers. Step is a court-movement counter: finish in the top two of your court and it ticks down; finish in the bottom two and it ticks up — based entirely on your last play date, however long ago that was.

Percentage breaks ties within a step: total points scored divided by maximum possible, over your last 90 games (~15 play dates), all weighted equally.

For Session 2, the top two on each court move up a court and the bottom two move down — the “2-up/2-back” rule.

11
Structural Weaknesses
STALE BY DESIGNStep reflects your last play date — which may be weeks or months old.
COURT-COUNT BLINDA step earned on a 5-court day penalizes you on a 3-court day. High-turnout days systematically punish; low-turnout days reward.
OPPONENT BLINDBottom-two on Court 1 against the strongest players costs the same as bottom-two on Court 3 against the weakest. Points scored are never adjusted for who you played or partnered with.
NO RECENCYA session three months ago counts exactly as much as last week’s.

Chapter Seven

The Net Result

We replayed the last 90 days of actual sessions and measured the skill spread inside each court — lower means the four players on a court are more evenly matched.

Under the current system, courts average a 491-point internal spread in Session 1. After the 2-up/2-back shuffle — which moves about 71% of all players — the spread widens to 556.

Read that again: the movement rule designed to sort players actually leaves courts less balanced than they started. Most of that movement is mechanical, not earned.

Chapter Eight

What We Tested — and How We Scored It

There is no shortage of ideas for assigning courts. To compare them fairly, we replayed the last 90 days of actual sessions — same players, same signups, same court counts — under each candidate method.

Every method gets two scores. Court tightness: how close in skill the four players on each court are — a smaller spread means fairer games. Shuffle: what share of players change courts between the two sessions — some movement is healthy; constant mechanical reshuffling is not.

Today’s system is the baseline to beat: a combined spread of 524, with 71% of players moving mid-morning.

15
Four Families of Ideas
1 · FIX THE STARTING LINEUPBuild Session 1 courts from player ratings instead of step and percentage. The morning starts fair; everything else stays exactly as it is.
2 · SOFTEN THE SHUFFLEKeep movement between sessions, but move one player up and one down per court instead of two. Less churn, same reward for winning.
3 · LET RESULTS DRIVE SESSION 2Re-rank everyone using their Session 1 results — weighted by who they faced — and rebuild the courts. A dial controls how strongly one morning moves you: steady, balanced, or fast.
4 · TARGETED SWAPS ONLYLeave courts alone except in special cases: two players nearly tied at a court boundary, or someone dramatically out-playing or under-playing their rating.

Technical Appendix · A

The Model Mechanics

For readers who want the nitty gritty. Every rating update follows one formula:

ΔR = K × (S − E) × M × D

S  = result (1 win, 0 loss)
E  = expected win prob = 1 / (1 + 10^((Ropp − Rteam)/400))
M = margin multiplier = min(ln(margin + 1), 2.0)
D  = recency weight, 25% → 100% across the window

Team ratings are the average of the two partners; all four players update after every rated game.

21
Technical Appendix · A, continued
NO-HISTORY-DRIFT WINDOWA player’s current rating is rebuilt by replaying only their last 60 rated games from a neutral 1,000 start. Long tenure carries zero legacy inflation; what you’ve done lately is what counts.
PROVISIONAL KK starts at 40 for a player’s first game and declines linearly to 20 by game 60, then holds. New players converge quickly; established ratings stay stable.
RECENCY DECAYWithin the 60-game window, weight ramps from 25% on the oldest game to 100% on the newest — the D in the formula. Yesterday’s games move your rating roughly four times as much as games about to age out, and nothing jumps when an old game finally leaves the window.

Technical Appendix · B

Data Hygiene & Display

WHAT COUNTSEvery posted shootout game since Jan 2022. Placeholder entries (tryouts, drop-ins), guest players, and flagged data errors are excluded from ratings; known name glitches are corrected at load.
LEADERBOARD QUALIFICATIONAt least 24 rated games within the past 180 days. Everyone else still carries a rating — shown with reduced confidence, pulled toward 1,000 in proportion to sample size and staleness.
FRESHNESSNo penalty for 90 days of inactivity; beyond that, a graduated confidence haircut up to 15%.

Technical Appendix · B, continued
EXPECTATION COMPRESSIONDisplayed win probabilities compress the rating gap by 0.85 before the logistic — matching observed SAM outcomes (57 / 66 / 78%) rather than theoretical chess curves.
VALIDATIONPredictions are checked against outcomes across the full pool every run. Gaps between individual actual and expected win rates reflect normal variance and close as games accumulate; aggregate calibration is what the model is tuned for.
SCENARIO REPLAYAssignment alternatives were tested against the last 90 days of real sessions — same signups, same court counts — not simulations of hypothetical players.
